Shellz quick verdict

Shared hosting versus DreamPress is the first decision to make

A single DreamHost score hides the biggest buyer question. A simple blog or small site may need only shared hosting. A business-critical WordPress site may value a more managed WordPress workflow. Shellz separates those paths so buyers do not pay for a higher product category without a clear reason.

My buying advice: For a small site, start by comparing the shared-hosting route with your actual needs. Move to DreamPress or another managed WordPress option only when the site needs the management features enough to justify the higher cost. Save both the first bill and renewal amount before payment.

Control panel and workflow

Check how you will manage the site after checkout

DreamHost uses its own account and hosting workflow. Buyers moving from cPanel should check domain, email, file, database, backup, and WordPress tasks before migration. Panel preference is a workflow issue, not a performance benchmark.

The workflow matters every week after purchase: creating email, changing DNS, restoring backups, moving files, working with databases, staging WordPress, and finding billing details. A familiar logo is not a substitute for checking those tasks.

Price and renewal

The first bill and the second bill are two different buying decisions

Shellz does not treat a promotional monthly number as the long-term cost. Before paying for DreamHost, save the plan name, billing term, full amount due today, renewal amount, renewal billing cycle, domain renewal, email cost or limits, backup rules, and optional add-ons.

  • 1. First totalRecord the actual amount charged today, not only the advertised monthly equivalent.
  • 2. Renewal amountCheck the price for the same plan after the promotional or first billing period.
  • 3. Included servicesSeparate domain, email, backups, security, migration, and premium support from the hosting price.
  • 4. Exit costKnow the refund terms, migration path, and what happens to a bundled domain or email service if you leave.
