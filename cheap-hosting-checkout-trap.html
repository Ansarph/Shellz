Quick answer

Cheap hosting is not the problem. Blind checkout is the problem.

Cheap web hosting can be a smart way to start a small website, blog, portfolio, or affiliate project. The mistake is looking only at the advertised monthly price and missing the total checkout cost, renewal price, billing term, domain fees, email, backups, security tools, and upgrade limits.

A hosting plan that looks like a few dollars per month can cost much more when you add the services your site actually needs.

Why this matters for beginners

When you start your first website, the hosting price is usually the first thing you notice. You may see a very low monthly price and think, “I can start my website for almost nothing.” Sometimes that is true. But only if you understand what is included and what is not included.

The real price of web hosting is not only the price shown on the landing page. The real price is the first checkout total, the renewal price, the billing period, the add-ons selected at checkout, the domain cost, the email cost, the backup cost, the security cost, and the cost to upgrade when your site grows.

This is where many beginners make mistakes. They buy quickly, then later discover that renewal is higher, email is not included, backups cost extra, or the low monthly price needs a long upfront payment.

Shellz rule

Do not ask only “Which host is cheapest?”

Ask: “What will this website cost me in the first year, and what will it cost me after renewal?”

That one question can save beginners from buying the wrong plan.

The 12 cheap hosting costs beginners miss

1. The advertised price may require long-term billing

Many hosting companies show a low monthly price, but that price may only apply when you choose a long contract. A page may show a low “per month” rate, but checkout may require 12, 24, 36, or 48 months upfront to get it.

This is not always bad. A longer term can reduce the average monthly cost. But beginners should understand the full checkout amount before entering payment details.

  • Is the price monthly or only shown as a monthly equivalent?
  • Do you need to pay yearly or multi-year upfront?
  • Can you choose a shorter term?
  • Does the price change if you select one month only?

2. Renewal price may be much higher

The first price is usually a promotional price. The renewal price is what you may pay after the first term ends. This is one of the biggest cheap hosting traps.

A plan may be affordable during the first year, but renewal can be much higher. If your site becomes important, moving later can be stressful, so you may end up paying the higher renewal price.

  • What is the renewal price?
  • When does renewal happen?
  • Will the domain renew separately?
  • Can you cancel before renewal?

3. A free domain may only be free for the first year

Some hosting plans include a free domain for the first year. That can be useful, but the domain usually renews at the normal domain renewal price later. Not every domain extension may be included.

Before relying on a free domain offer, check eligible extensions, renewal price, domain privacy, and transfer rules.

4. Domain privacy may cost extra

Domain privacy hides personal contact information from public WHOIS records. Some registrars include it free, while others charge extra. For personal blogs, small business websites, and affiliate sites, this can matter.

5. Email may not be included

Beginners often assume that buying hosting automatically gives them a professional email address like info@yourdomain.com. Sometimes it does. Sometimes it does not.

Some hosts include basic email. Others charge extra. Some website builders require a separate email service such as Google Workspace, Microsoft 365, Zoho, or another provider.

For a simple explanation, read Domain vs Hosting vs Email.

6. Backups may cost extra

Backups are important because websites break. A plugin update, wrong file edit, malware issue, or accidental deletion can damage a site. Some hosting plans include automatic backups. Others charge extra for daily backups or easy restore tools.

For a serious website, backups are not optional. Check whether backups are daily, weekly, manual, free to restore, and stored long enough.

7. Security tools may be added at checkout

Some hosting checkouts include optional security add-ons such as malware scanning, site protection, premium SSL, or security monitoring. Security matters, but beginners should not blindly accept every upsell.

Some basic security features may already be included. Some add-ons may be useful only for larger or higher-risk websites.

8. SSL may be free, but premium SSL may be an upsell

Most normal websites need standard SSL so the site loads with HTTPS. Many hosts provide free SSL. At checkout, some providers may offer premium SSL. That does not mean free SSL is useless.

For a beginner blog, small business website, portfolio, or affiliate site, free SSL is often enough.

9. Website migration may not be free

If you already have a website somewhere else, moving it to a new host may require migration. Some providers include free migration. Others charge for it. Some only provide migration for selected plans or selected website types.

10. “Unlimited” may still have fair-use limits

Many shared hosting plans advertise unlimited websites, unlimited bandwidth, or unlimited storage. This does not always mean unlimited in the way beginners imagine.

Shared hosting usually has fair-use rules, inode limits, CPU limits, memory limits, and performance restrictions. For a small site, this may not matter. For a growing site, it can matter a lot.

11. Intro coupon codes may not work on every plan

Coupon codes are useful, but not every coupon works on every product. A hosting coupon may apply only to new customers, selected plans, annual billing, first term only, or specific products.

Some deals are auto-applied and do not need a code. This is why Shellz separates real Show code buttons from Get deal buttons.

12. Upgrading later may cost more than choosing the right plan first

The cheapest plan is not always the best plan. If your website needs more storage, more traffic, better speed, staging, backups, ecommerce tools, or better support, you may need to upgrade later.

Starting cheap is fine. Starting too limited can waste time.

Cheap hosting is good when you use it correctly

Cheap hosting is not a scam by itself. Many websites begin on affordable hosting. Cheap hosting is good for first websites, personal blogs, small business sites, portfolios, test projects, beginner affiliate sites, simple WordPress websites, and learning how hosting works.

Cheap hosting becomes a problem when beginners expect premium performance, unlimited growth, advanced support, daily backups, business email, ecommerce power, and zero renewal increase from the lowest plan.

The right mindset is simple: start affordable, but do not start blind.

Best beginner buying strategy

Step 1: Decide your website type

A blog, business site, coupon site, portfolio, and ecommerce store do not need the same hosting. A simple portfolio can use GitHub Pages or a website builder. A blog or affiliate site is usually better on WordPress hosting. A store needs ecommerce-ready hosting or an ecommerce platform.

For platform choice, read AI Website Builder vs WordPress Hosting vs GitHub Pages.

Step 2: Choose the cheapest plan that is not too limited

Do not buy the biggest plan because you are excited. But also do not buy the cheapest plan if it blocks your basic needs. Your first plan should include enough storage, free SSL, easy WordPress installation if needed, clear renewal pricing, basic support, and a simple upgrade path.

Step 3: Avoid unnecessary checkout add-ons

At checkout, remove anything you do not understand. Then add back only what you truly need. For a very small beginner site, you may not need every premium add-on on day one.

Step 4: Save the renewal date

After buying hosting, immediately save the renewal date in your calendar. This helps you avoid surprise renewal charges.

Step 5: Publish useful content before upgrading

Do not spend too much before your website has real pages, real visitors, and a clear purpose. For Shellz-style websites, publish helpful guides first, then upgrade after you see which pages get impressions in Google Search Console.

Good buyer vs bad buyer

Bad buyer

“I saw the low monthly price, clicked buy, added every checkout option, and did not check renewal. Now the total is higher than expected.”

Good buyer

“I checked the first-term price, renewal price, domain cost, email cost, backups, SSL, refund policy, and coupon eligibility. I bought only what I needed for the first version of my site.”

What Shellz recommends

For beginners, Shellz recommends comparing hosting by total cost, not only by advertised price. A good cheap hosting plan should be affordable at checkout, clear about renewal pricing, easy to use, good enough for your website type, not overloaded with required add-ons, and simple to upgrade later.

If a hosting deal looks too cheap, do not reject it immediately. Just verify the full cost.

FAQ

Cheap hosting checkout questions

Is cheap web hosting bad?

No. Cheap hosting can be a smart starting point for small websites. The risk is buying without checking renewal price, add-ons, backups, email, and plan limits.

Why does the price increase at checkout?

The checkout may include a longer billing term, domain add-ons, email, backups, security tools, premium SSL, or optional services selected by default.

Should I buy all hosting add-ons?

No. Buy only what your first version needs. You can often add extra services later after your site has real content and traffic.

Are hosting coupons always real?

No. Some codes expire, some apply only to selected plans, and some discounts are auto-applied without a code. Shellz uses Show code for real codes and Get deal for auto-applied offers.
